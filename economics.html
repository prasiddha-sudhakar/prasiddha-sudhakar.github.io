Macroeconomics

Introduction

Macroeconomics explores the economy as a whole. In this topic, you will be learning about:

Overall level of Economic Activity

Here, we will begin by exploring the circular flow of income model. In order to do that, we will start with a few basic definitions.

Factors of Production

There are 4 few factors of production:

  • Land
    • These are raw materials and primary commodities
  • Capital
    • Means to produce products, such as machinery
  • Labor
    • Physical and Mental exertion
  • Entrepreneurship
    • The one who starts companies

Measuring Economic Activity

There are 3 main approaches:

Expenditure

adds up all spending to buy final goods and services produced within a country over a time period

Income

adds up all income earned by the factors of production that produce all goods and services within a country over a time period

Output

calculates the value of all final goods and services produced in a country over a time period.

Expenditure Approach

This can be broken down into the simple calculation:

\[\text{C + I + G + (X-M)} = multiplier\]

Where:

  • C: Consumption
  • I: Investment
  • G: Government Spending
  • X-M: Net exports (Exports [X] - Imports[M])
GDP: the market value of all final goods and services produced in a country over a time period

Where final refers to goods and services ready for final use, and can be contrasted with intermediate goods and services, or those purchased as inputs for the production of final goods.

Macroeconomic Objectives

In IB Economics, you will explore 5 key macroeconomic objectives that economists would like to achieve. These are:
  1. Steady Economic Growth
  2. Low Prices and Inflation
  3. Low Levels of Unemployment
  4. Balanced Balance of payments

    (This will explored in more detailed during International Economics)

  5. Equitable Income Distribution

Equilibrium Positions

To understand the various equilibrium positions, we must consider the concept of Yf (aka. Full Economic Potential!)

Yf: An economy's full potential GDP, also the full employment level of real output

Deflationary Gaps

Deflationary Gap: Exists where the actual level of output (Y) is less than the potential/full employment level of output (Yf)
Neoclassical

In the Neoclassical Model, an deflationary gap will correct itself. Here are the reasons why:

  1. As shown in figure 1.2, the economy is currently in a recession (and currently has high levels of unemployment as Y is less than Yf)
  2. However, in the long run, as Y is less than Yf, the economy is not producing as much real output as it potentially could. FOr this reason, firms will demand less factors of production as it is not ncessary.
  3. This leads to a fall in the prices of factors of production, which in fact, causes SRAS to shift to the right
  4. As a result, the economy returns to equiibrium and closes the deflationary gap by itself
Keynsian

In the Keynsian model, a deflationary gap won't close itself. This is because:

  1. FOPs and wage rates aren't flexible. They are totally fixed
  2. Low profits and losses because of high costs
  3. As a result, the economy is stuck in a deflationary gap
  4. What needs to happen? The government must intervene to manually move AD to the right position. This will be explored in Demand Side Polivies (Fiscal & Monetary Policy)

Inflationary Gaps

Inflationary Gap: Exists where the potential/full employment level of output (Yf) is less than the actual level of output (Y)
Neoclassical

In the Neoclassical Model, an inflationary gap will correct itself. Here are the reasons why:

  1. As shown in figure 1.3, the economy is currently overworking itself by performing greater than it's potential GDP
  2. For this reason, workers will need more compensation
  3. Or, more workers will be needed, driving up the demand for workers. However, these are scarce resources
  4. This drives the cost of factors of production up, causing SRAS to fall
  5. This leaves the economy return back to Yf

These can be used to close inflationary gaps by increasing taxation and lowering government spending, directly targetting investment and consumption as components of AD

The Keynsian Multiplier

The fundamental argument behind the multiplier is to encourage the use of government spending during a recession.

Let's say that there's an increase in one of the components of AD, causing it to shift to the right. We can see that the final increase in real output/GDP will most likely be greater than the initial increase in expenditures.

\[multiplier = {\text{change in real GDP} \over \text{initial change in expenditure}}\]

So... how do we calculate the multiplier?

First, there are a few important terms that we must define a few key terms

Marginal Propensity to Consume [MPC]: Is the extra amount of consumption which occurs as a result of a given increase in income
Marginal Propensity to Save [MPS]: Is the extra amount of saving which occurs as a result of a given increase in income
Marginal Propensity to Import [MPM]: Is the extra amount of imports which occurs as a result of a given increase in income
Marginal Rate of Taxation [MRT]: Is the extra amount of tax which occurs as a result of a given increase in income
Marginal Propensity to Withdraw [MPW]: MPS + MPM + MRT

Let's denote the multiplier as k

\[k = {1 \over \ (1 - MPC)} = {1 \over \ (MPW)} = {1 \over \ (MPS + MPM + MRT)}\]

What's the significance of using the multiplier?

It's a good indicator to the government on how much government spending is necessary to return the economy to Yf

For example, in a recession, MPC is low as consumer confidence is low. This means that governments much spend more to get the economy back into shape

Automatic Stabilisers

Automatic Stabilisers: Factors that automatically work toward stabilizing the economy by reducing the short-term fluctuations of the business cycle
Examples of Automatic Stabilizers
Progressive Income Tax

In the peak of the business cycle, incomes rise as the economy is booming. As incomes rise, the government is able to have higher sources of revenue due to progressive income tax. This means that consumers have lower disposible incomes, causing consumption to fall. This controls the economy when it's booming.

On the other hand, during the truph of the business cycle, incomes fall. Henceforth, the government will have lower tax revenues and consumers will have higher disposible incomes. This causes consumption to increase and stimulates the economy during a recession.

Unemployment Benefits

In an economic boom, unemployment benefits are reduced as less people are unemployed. As a result, consumption increases less than if unemployment benefits existed. This causes consumption to drop and ease the economy in a boom

The opposite case is when there's a recession. If there is more unemployment, the government will give out more unemployment benefits. With this in mind, consumption will slightly increase and improve the economy.

Crowding Out

Crowding out is a neoclassical concept

Crowding Out: When the government gets money from loans, thus stopping others from getting loans and deprives businesses from getting money.
The basic idea of crowding out: As the government spends more money, it reduces the amount of resources available for private firms

A fundamental assumption about the government is that the government needs to borrow money and that there is a budget deficit. We assume that the government needs to borrow money due to a deflationary gap; there will be a fall in incomes and so the government will have lower revenues from taxes

  1. The initial equiibrium is where DLF = SLF and Q quantity of loanable funds is lent and borrowed at interest rate r
  2. Due to the government increasing the demand for the loanable funds, they inadvertently drive up the interest rate as loanable funds become more scarce
  3. This shows how as the government spends more money, this reduces the amoutn of resources available for private firms - aka; "crowds them out"

Monetary Policy

Monetary Policy is a type of Demand Side Policy
Monetary Policy: Government policies aiming to manipulate the supply of money and interest rate in order to change the level of AD

First of all - what is the central bank?

A central bank is a government financial institution that has several key responsibilities:

  1. Banker to the government
  2. Banked to commercial banks
  3. Regulator of commercial banks
  4. Conduct monetary policy

Determination of interest rate

You can think about this in terms of demand and supply with quantity of money on the x axis and interest rate on the y axis

What would happen if we decided to increase the supply of money?

Firstly, we would need to look at how the demand and supply curves would look like for money based on elasticities

  • Demand: This is the same old downward sloping demand curve
    • As the interest rate falls, the demand for money (for loans) increases
  • Supply: This would be perfectly inelastic (straight line)
    • The supply of money is not affected by the interest rate
    • This is a fixed rate determined by the central bank

In IB Economics, we do not focus on the central bank manipulating the supply of money. Instead, we focus on the manipulation of the interest rate

The role of monetary policy

Monetary policy aims to target the levels of investment and consumption in the economy and manipulate AD

Expansionary monetary policy

When cutting interest rates, individuals and firms have accumulated less interest therefore they are more incentivized to take out loans

This would shift AD to the right

Contractionary monetary policy

When increasing interest rates, individuals and firms have accumulated more interest therefore they are less incentivized to take out loans

This would shift AD to the left

Targetting inflation using monetary policy

Inflation targetting, according to the IMF, is defined as:

‘... the public announcement of medium-term numerical targets for infl ation with an institutional commitment by the monetary authority to achieve these targets. Additional key features include increased communication with the public and the markets about the plans and objectives of monetary policymakers and increased accountability of the central bank for attaining its infl ation objectives.’

Advantages of targetting inflation:

  • Lower rates are achievable
  • Stability in low rates of inflation
    • Due to this, economic decision making becomes significantly easier
    • This makes it easier to anticipate future inflation

Disadvantages of targetting inflation:

  • Lower rates are achievable
